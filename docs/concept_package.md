# Toybox Dominion Internal Concept Bible

Working title: **Toybox Dominion**

Status: internal canon. The title is a working label, not final branding.

One-line premise: in a pristine house where the family never returned, old toys turn the last game they remember into law, racing for control of rooms, routes, protection, exile, and the future of every toy left behind.

## Core Concept

The house is clean, quiet, and untouched. It is not ruined, dirty, or post-apocalyptic. The unease comes from how ready everything still looks, as if the family might return at any moment, even though no one has.

The toys know they are toys. They understand play, shelves, storage, scuffs, repairs, missing pieces, favorite status, display, and being left behind. What they do not understand is the silence. Some believe the family left. Some believe the family will come back. Some doubt the family was ever what the older toys claim. The game never confirms the truth.

Over time, the toys claimed rooms as territories. Each room became a faction built around a different answer to abandonment:

- Kitchen: law, ceremony, and order.
- Bedroom: shelter, memory, and waiting.
- Sandbox: strength, tribute, and command.
- Garden: adaptation, terrain, and survival.
- Glam Closet: visibility, presentation, and status.
- Outdoor Playground: movement, risk, and freedom.
- Playroom: spectacle, champions, and noise.
- Attic: secrets, tricks, and broken rules.

Direct war is taboo because broken toys cannot be replaced. Racing is dangerous, political proxy conflict. Sabotage, traps, intimidation, and risky courses are tolerated inside race law, but openly trying to break another toy is treated as a crime against the house.

## Why Racing?

The child once raced the toys against each other during play. That was how many of them understood being chosen: motion, cheering, collisions, rivalries, winners, rematches, and the feeling that someone cared enough to set them in motion.

After the family disappeared, racing became the only shared rule the toys still remembered. It started as a way to preserve the old play pattern. It hardened into government.

The toys race because racing is the last language of play they all recognize. A race result can settle borders, determine safe passage, assign punishment, grant protection, or crown the Champion of the House. The old game became law.

## The Seventh Night

For the first few nights, most toys believed the family would return. The house was too clean for panic. The lights, furniture, rooms, and shelves still seemed ready.

By the seventh night, no one had returned. No footsteps, no voices, no opened doors, no hands reaching for favorites. The toys gathered for the first emergency council near the old play area.

The council split around three questions:

- Should they wait?
- Should they search?
- Should they claim rooms before someone else does?

The first new race was meant to settle the argument without breaking anyone. Instead, it gave the winner authority and taught every faction a harsher lesson: whoever controls the race controls the house.

The Seventh Night is the founding wound of the current cold war. Every racer remembers it differently. The facts are stable; the meaning is not.

## House Rule

The Champion of the House does not gain magic power. The champion gains practical authority:

- Control over room borders.
- Control over safe routes and shortcuts.
- Control over who may cross faction territory.
- Control over exile, shelter, and punishment.
- Control over sanctioned race terms.
- Control over which toys are protected by house law.

Each campaign ending is an alternate outcome showing what happens when one toy's belief becomes house policy.

## House And Yard Spatial Canon

The house and yard should read as one connected, pristine, uncanny domestic world, not as disconnected track boxes. The concept floor plan is the current spatial reference for room adjacency, route logic, and stage relationships.

![Racer House + Yard Concept Floor Plan](story_bible/concepts/floor_plans/racer_house_yard_concept_floor_plan.png)

Figma source: [Racer House + Yard Concept Floor Plan](https://www.figma.com/design/odffTKsyMsVDXC3TltryUG)

The plan is concept canon, not construction documentation. It defines relationships, emotional geography, and route fantasy; individual Godot scenes may compress or exaggerate distances for playability, camera readability, and mobile performance.

Spatial relationships:

- The backyard sits north of the house and holds the outdoor territories: Garden, Outdoor Playground, and Sandbox.
- The patio/deck is the hinge between domestic order and outdoor play. It links Kitchen, Playroom, and the backyard without making the world feel like separate rooms on a menu.
- Kitchen + Pantry sits on the rear left of the main floor and remains the measured interior quality baseline.
- Playroom sits on the rear right of the main floor, sharing visual and route logic with the patio, playground, and toy arena fantasy.
- Dining/Hall, Living Room, Entry/Stairs, Garage/Service, Front Walk, Driveway, and Street form the grounded house shell around the faction spaces.
- Bedroom and Glam Closet sit upstairs as adjacent territories connected through Entry/Stairs.
- Attic Access / Storage sits above the upper floor and carries the hidden-route, storage, and prank fantasy.

World-building rules:

- Keep the house pristine and uncanny even when factions decorate, claim, or weaponize the space.
- Cluster wet systems around Kitchen, washer, sink, hose, and Garden so water effects feel physically motivated.
- Keep Sandbox dry, tactile, and visually distinct from Garden and Patio.
- Preserve open lawn or equivalent negative space in outdoor layouts for camera clearance, recovery, and route readability.
- Treat doors, stairs, patio thresholds, shelves, counters, and windowsills as story-bearing route connectors.
- Use adjacency to make faction politics legible: Kitchen law faces Playroom spectacle across the patio, Garden survival presses against the yard edge, Sandbox strength holds a visible outdoor claim, and upstairs rooms feel protected but not isolated.

## Character Canon

### Tuggs

- Home faction: Bedroom / The Soft Room.
- Public belief: the family will come back, and every toy must be kept safe, clean, and ready until that day.
- Private wound: Tuggs cannot accept that the last time he was not chosen might have meant nothing.
- Rival: Rexx.
- Alliance: Sir Clink, through the Preservation Pact.
- Ending direction: The Waiting House.

Tuggs is the faithful one. He shelters damaged, scared, and low-status toys because no toy should be discarded before the family returns. His care is real, but it can become captivity. Toys who want to leave, change, or stop waiting threaten the fragile hope his faction is built around.

### Rexx

- Home faction: Sandbox / The Sand Throne.
- Public belief: no one is coming. Weak toys invented waiting because they fear power.
- Private wound: Rexx remembers being played with roughly, as a monster or obstacle, and decided fear is better than neglect.
- Rival: Tuggs.
- Alliance: Moko, through the Survival Pact.
- Ending direction: The Strong House.

Rexx believes the house is a power vacuum. If no hand returns to choose a favorite, then strength must choose. He protects what is useful, absorbs what is weak, and turns safety into tribute.

### Sir Clink

- Home faction: Kitchen / The Kitchen Court.
- Public belief: the old games had rules, so the house must have law.
- Private wound: Sir Clink failed to hold the first council together and now overcorrects through ceremony, borders, and judgment.
- Rival: Popper.
- Alliance: Tuggs, through the Preservation Pact.
- Ending direction: The Ordered House.

Sir Clink wants law to protect the toys from panic. He believes race law can keep the house from breaking itself. His justice is sincere, but his need for order can turn disobedience into exile.

### Popper

- Home faction: Attic / The Attic Pranks.
- Public belief: the rules failed the moment the family disappeared.
- Private wound: Popper was boxed, shelved, or ignored before the silence and sees proper play as a lie.
- Rival: Sir Clink.
- Alliance: none. Popper is the unaffiliated wildcard.
- Ending direction: The Unruled House.

Popper treats every rule as a toy waiting to be pulled apart. He makes deals with every faction, exposes private wounds before important races, and refuses to belong long enough to be responsible. He is often right about hypocrisy and often cruel about proving it.

### Velva

- Home faction: Glam Closet / The Mirror Court.
- Public belief: a toy that is not seen is already disappearing.
- Private wound: Velva remembers being arranged, admired, and then left untouched, so attention feels like survival.
- Rival: Dash.
- Alliance: Slammo, through the Spectacle Pact.
- Ending direction: The Display House.

Velva believes visibility is power. She gives forgotten toys roles, polish, ceremony, and a place in the house's display. Her court can restore meaning, but it ranks toys by shine, usefulness, beauty, and audience.

### Dash

- Home faction: Outdoor Playground / The Open Run.
- Public belief: waiting is how toys become furniture.
- Private wound: Dash believes he nearly reached an open route once, but another toy stopped him for his own good.
- Rival: Velva.
- Alliance: temporary route deals, never permanent membership.
- Ending direction: The Open House.

Dash distrusts every faction because every faction eventually builds a gate. He makes practical alliances to open routes, then leaves before help becomes ownership. His freedom is real, but it can abandon toys who cannot survive open roads alone.

### Moko

- Home faction: Garden / The Green Claim.
- Public belief: the house is not a home anymore. It is territory.
- Private wound: Moko adapted first and was judged as savage by toys still clinging to old house etiquette.
- Rival: Slammo.
- Alliance: Rexx, through the Survival Pact.
- Ending direction: The Wild House.

Moko respects patterns, terrain, limits, and endurance. He does not want conquest for its own sake, but he believes nostalgia is dangerous. His faction survives by adapting to what the house is now, not what the toys wish it still was.

### Slammo

- Home faction: Playroom / The Arena.
- Public belief: if the house is quiet, make it loud enough to matter.
- Private wound: Slammo was once the center of a huge play session and has been chasing that feeling ever since.
- Rival: Moko.
- Alliance: Velva, through the Spectacle Pact.
- Ending direction: The Champion House.

Slammo turns fear into events, titles, chants, and rivalries. He gives the house energy, but he struggles to believe anything matters unless someone is watching.

## Rivalry Canon

### Tuggs vs Rexx: Waiting vs Seizing

Old betrayal: Rexx broke from the waiting faction and claimed the Sandbox, taking scared toys with him through force, intimidation, or promises of protection.

Tuggs remembers Rexx abandoning the vulnerable. Rexx remembers Tuggs asking everyone to sit still and decay. Their races ask whether safety comes from shelter or strength.

### Sir Clink vs Popper: Law vs Mockery

Old betrayal: Popper sabotaged an early council race to prove the new law could be cheated.

Sir Clink remembers it as treason. Popper remembers it as proof that honorable toys were already turning ceremony into control. Their races ask whether rules protect the house or disguise power.

### Velva vs Dash: Being Seen vs Being Free

Old betrayal: Velva helped block Dash from opening routes beyond accepted room borders because she feared he would scatter the house.

Dash remembers her turning freedom into a staged display with gates. Velva remembers him trying to abandon everyone for a fantasy of escape. Their races ask whether toys need witnesses or open roads.

### Moko vs Slammo: Survival vs Spectacle

Old betrayal: Slammo turned an emergency survival race into an arena event, exposing Moko's faction to ridicule or danger.

Moko remembers Slammo wasting fear as entertainment. Slammo remembers Moko trying to make the house silent, grim, and joyless. Their races ask whether the house survives through adaptation or shared myth.

## Alliance Canon

Alliances have mixed motives. Each alliance has a sincere emotional overlap and a political advantage. They are not simple friendships.

### Tuggs + Sir Clink: Preservation Pact

Shared belief: the house needs protection, memory, and rules while the family is gone.

Emotional reason: Tuggs trusts Sir Clink to defend weaker toys. Sir Clink respects Tuggs' loyalty.

Political reason: Bedroom shelter plus Kitchen law creates a powerful safe-house bloc.

Tension: Tuggs wants to wait for the family. Sir Clink wants to govern even if they never return.

Break point: Sir Clink exiles a toy for order, or Tuggs shelters someone who violated race law.

### Rexx + Moko: Survival Pact

Shared belief: the house is no longer safe, and soft hopes will get toys controlled or forgotten.

Emotional reason: both respect strength, endurance, and adaptation.

Political reason: Sandbox force plus Garden terrain control makes them hard to invade.

Tension: Rexx wants domination. Moko wants adaptation without unnecessary conquest.

Break point: Rexx uses Moko's survival logic as justification for tyranny.

### Velva + Slammo: Spectacle Pact

Shared belief: attention gives toys meaning, and the house must be made loud, visible, and memorable.

Emotional reason: both understand performance as survival.

Political reason: Glam Closet image-making plus Playroom arena culture can sway neutral toys.

Tension: Velva choreographs status. Slammo wants raw crowd energy and personal glory.

Break point: Velva tries to manage Slammo as a symbol, or Slammo ruins her image control for applause.

### Dash + Temporary Route Deals

Dash does not belong to a stable pair. He makes practical agreements when routes need opening.

Most natural temporary ally: Moko, because both distrust waiting and fixed borders.

Most explosive temporary ally: Slammo, because both love speed and risk but disagree on performance.

Tension: Dash rejects any pact that starts to feel like ownership.

Break point: any ally tries to turn freedom into policy, branding, or territory.

### Popper: Unaffiliated Wildcard

Popper makes deals with every bloc but treats alliances as toys' favorite lie.

He helps Tuggs embarrass Rexx, helps Rexx expose Sir Clink's hypocrisy, helps Velva sabotage Dash's freedom myth, and helps Dash when chaos opens routes. He never joins a side long enough to be responsible for it.

## Ending Canon

Endings are alternate outcomes. No ending confirms whether the family returns, left, vanished, or ever existed as remembered. Each ending shows what improves when one belief rules the house and what becomes oppressive under that same belief.

### Tuggs: The Waiting House

Tuggs becomes Keeper of the Return. No toy may be discarded, exiled, or left alone while the family might still return.

The house becomes safer. Damaged and frightened toys are sheltered in the Bedroom. Rexx is contained, not destroyed, because even dangerous toys must be preserved.

The cost is stillness. The house freezes around waiting, and toys who want freedom or change are treated as threats to hope. The final image is every toy cleaned, arranged, and ready, facing the door in silence.

### Rexx: The Strong House

Rexx becomes First Claw of the House. Every room owes strength, service, or tribute to the champion.

No toy is ignored. Weak factions receive protection from outside threats and internal chaos.

The cost is ownership. Fear keeps the house unified. Tuggs' refuge remains only by Rexx's permission. The final image is a network of conquered tracks cut through every room, with Rexx watching from the Sandbox ridge.

### Sir Clink: The Ordered House

Sir Clink becomes Crown Marshal of the Old Game. All disputes must be settled by sanctioned races under Kitchen Court rules.

Borders stabilize, sabotage decreases, and weaker toys can appeal to race law.

The cost is obedience. Toys who reject the court lose access, status, or movement. Popper is not broken; he is sentenced to race forever under rules he despises. The final image is polished gates and banners across the house, beautiful and controlled.

### Popper: The Unruled House

Popper refuses the crown. His only law is that every law must be breakable, every route must have a hidden route, and every ruler must be mocked.

Exiles return. Borders open. Toys trapped by status find new freedom.

The cost is trust. No agreement feels permanent, and former protections vanish with the court structures Popper humiliates. The final image is attic strings, secret ramps, and prank gates running through the whole pristine house.

### Velva: The Display House

Velva becomes First Star of the Mirror Court. Every toy must be placed, presented, and given a role in the house's grand display.

Forgotten toys become visible again. Rooms gain ceremony, beauty, and identity.

The cost is rank. Toys without shine, story, or usefulness sink to the edges. Dash is given a glorious role he never asked for. The final image is the house glowing like a showroom, every toy perfectly seen and quietly sorted.

### Dash: The Open House

Dash becomes the First Free Runner. No room may close its borders, and no toy may be forced to stay where it was placed.

Movement returns. Hidden routes, shortcuts, and crossings connect the whole house.

The cost is exposure. Safety becomes optional, and some toys are not strong enough for total freedom. Velva loses control of display and status, but her court becomes more alive. The final image is doors, ramps, windowsills, and rails connected into one reckless route.

### Moko: The Wild House

Moko becomes Root-Keeper of the Green Claim. Every room must adapt to the house as it is now, not as it was remembered.

Toys learn resilience, repair, terrain, and survival beyond old play rituals.

The cost is hardness. Softness, ceremony, and nostalgia are treated as weaknesses. Slammo's arena is overgrown with practical hazards and stripped of empty spectacle. The final image is the pristine house crossed with improvised paths, survival marks, and quiet vigilance.

### Slammo: The Champion House

Slammo becomes House Champion Eternal. Every conflict becomes a show, every room gets a title, and every toy gets a crowd.

Energy returns. The silence is replaced with competition, chants, events, and shared spectacle.

The cost is performance. Toys who cannot perform become background, and feeling alive depends on applause. Moko is forced into the arena logic he despises, proving survival can be made entertaining. The final image is the Playroom lighting up as the whole house roars for a champion who cannot bear quiet.

## Writing Rules

- Write as internal canon, not external marketing copy.
- Keep the family mystery unresolved.
- Keep the house pristine and uncanny.
- Keep every character morally mixed.
- Let rivals remain ideologically alive after defeat.
- Use racing as political ritual, not just sport.
- Use toy realities in language: shelves, bins, scuffs, favorite status, storage, repairs, display, missing pieces, and being left behind.
- Do not make direct violence normal. The race is the battlefield because the toys are irreplaceable.
